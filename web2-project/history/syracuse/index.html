Syracuse's Story!

Lyncourt, being part of the greater Syracuse area, takes pride in highlighting significant aspects of local Syracuse history. One notable historical feature of the region is the Erie Canal, which played a crucial role in the economic development of New York State. The Erie Canal Museum in downtown Syracuse preserves a portion of the canal and houses a replica of the ships that once navigated its waters, offering a glimpse into the 19th-century trade routes that connected the region to the Great Lakes and beyond.

Syracuse is also known for its abundant natural salt reserves, which stem from its geological history as part of a former saltwater lake. These salt deposits became central to the city’s industrial growth in the 19th century, earning Syracuse the nickname "The Salt City." To honor this heritage, the Salt Museum at Onondaga Lake Park, shown in the picture above, showcases the tools and techniques once used to extract salt from the nearby brine springs. This industry was so significant that it attracted workers from all over, helping the city grow.

One culinary legacy tied to Syracuse’s salt industry is the city's most iconic dish: Salt Potatoes. This simple yet beloved dish, made by boiling Yukon Gold potatoes in heavily salted water, was originally a staple of the Irish salt workers who labored in the region's salt mines, and I would highly recommend giving them a try! In Wegmans, they sell pre-packed bags labeled "Salt Potatoes" with Yukon Gold potatoes and the appropriate amount of salt.
